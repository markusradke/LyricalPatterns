\begin{abstract}
Recent advances in natural language processing have improved lyrics-based music genre classification through large language models, yet such black box systems obscure the actual linguistic style differences between music genres. Interpretable approaches in computational musicology and MIR, meanwhile, often relied on either formal structural analysis of lyrics or on topic or sentiment analyses. However, genre-typical formulaic expressions, though deemed relevant in popular music studies, have hardly been investigated at all. Our study contributes to filling this gap by uncovering genre discriminative lyrical style patterns—topics, sentiments, and formulaic expressions—in fifty years of Anglophone German popular music. Using the POPTRAG corpus and \textit{Discogs} genre annotations for 126,042 tracks with English lyrics, we apply the fightin' words method to identify statistically distinctive n-grams across genres. We then identify latent topics, sentiments, and types of formulaic expressions from these via structural topic modeling using training corpus target genres as prevalence covariates. Resulting interpretable lyrics features are employed in machine-learning models that reveal the function of stylistic markers for specific popular music genres. Our transparent ML method approaches the predictive performance of a transfer-learned DistilBERT classifier while offering conceptual insight into how lyrics encode genre identity. 
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
Popular music does not resonate with audiences through sound alone. Lyrics shape emotional interpretation, social meaning, and collective memory \cite{davis1985pop, frithWhySongsHave1986}, making them a central component of the listening experience and a rich object of study for Computational Musicology (CM) and Music Information Retrieval (MIR) \cite{watanabeLyricsInformationProcessing2020}. Because lyrics are textual material, they allow large-scale analysis across songs and genres, complementing audio-based approaches with an explicit linguistic perspective on popular music. This makes them especially useful for studying music genre differences, since genres organize songs through recurring stylistic markers and socio-cultural conventions \cite{fabbriTheoryMusicalGenres1982}, many of which are encoded in lyrics \cite{paradacabaleiroSongLyricsHave2024, carboneStatusMarkersPopular2024, bettiLargeScaleAnalysis2023, schedlGenreDifferencesSong2019, squiresGenreLinguisticExpectation2019}. To this end, lyrics-based genre recognition can serve as a research tool for popular music studies: by predicting genre from textual features, it can uncover which lyrical patterns most strongly differentiate genres and how these patterns vary across the popular music landscape \cite{tsaptsinosLyricsBasedMusicGenre2017, mayerRHYMESTYLEFEATURES2008a}.

Recent research at the intersection of MIR and Natural Language Processing (NLP) has leveraged deep learning architectures and word embeddings to employ lyrics for genre classification \cite{liMusicGenreClassification2023,akalpLanguageRepresentationModels2021, dearaujolimaBrazilianLyricsBasedMusic2020, tsaptsinosLyricsBasedMusicGenre2017}. Pretrained LLM models, such as BERT \cite{devlinBERTPretrainingDeep2019} and its derivatives (e.g., RoBERTa \cite{liuRoBERTaRobustlyOptimized2019} and DistilBERT \cite{sanhDistilBERTDistilledVersion2020}) have become widely used in NLP classification tasks, offering strong predictive performance but limited transparency \cite{gardaziBERTApplicationsNatural2025}. Their embeddings compress complex semantic and syntactic information into high-dimensional spaces whose dimensions lack human-interpretable meaning. Consequently, while such models excel at prediction, they provide little understanding of how linguistic patterns shape genre distinctions. While there exist approaches to trace black-box model decisions back to individual humam-readable token through attention visualisation \cite{skrljExploringNeuralLanguage2021,tsaptsinosLyricsBasedMusicGenre2017}, they do not mitigate semantic bias during embedding of tokens \cite{muzambiMultidimensionalMIRAnalysis2026, benderDangersStochasticParrots2021} and insights remain at the sparse representation level of individual tokens. This interpretability gap limits both scholarly attempts to connect lyrical style with musical culture and applied uses of lyrics-based classifiers, where understanding model behavior is essential for debugging, bias detection, and trustworthy deployment \cite{sturmSimpleMethodDetermine2014,rudinStopExplainingBlack2019}.


Conversely, interpretable approaches to lyrics-based music genre analysis in CM and MIR have so far emphasized stylistic features that relate to formal or structural aspects of lyrics, such as rhyme features, repetition features (e.g., repeated line ratio, echoisms), textual statistics (e.g., word count, POS-tag frequencies) or lexical descriptors (e.g., complexity,  readability, type-token ratio, slang frequency, metaphor density) \cite{muzambiMultidimensionalMIRAnalysis2026,mayerlComparingLyricsFeatures2020, schedlGenreDifferencesSong2019,fell2014lyrics}. While such features can capture important stylistic tendencies, they might fail to fully capture genre identity that is also shaped by patterns of certain topics, sentiments and linguistic formulas typical for the music producing scenes \cite{frithWhySongsHave1986}. Together these aspects are called \textit{lyrical style} \cite{kreyerStylePopSong2007}. 

In this contribution, we conceptualize style-related genre-discriminating lyrical patterns as recurrent linguistic structures that are strongly associated with specific genres and that relate to the meaning of lyrics. These include topics (What are the lyrics about?, e.g., love, doubt, drugs), sentiments (With what affect do lyrics talk about something?, e.g., romantic, apocalyptic, affirmative), and formulaic expressions (genre-specific slang, typical collocations, and vocalizations; e.g., ``jah'', ``get down on it'', ``oh la la''). Notably, we understand formulaic expressions as a broad category that not only includes multi-word expressions like commonly-used collocations and idioms \cite{wrayFunctionsFormulaicLanguage2000, aminDatadrivenIdentificationIdioms2021}, but also single words that show formulaic usage in music lyrics and function as style markers, such as slang words or words with spelling variations \cite{fell2014lyrics,kreyerStylePopSong2007}, highly common words that appear as lexical rather than topical \cite{fell2014lyrics}, and single-word vocalizations \cite{knaufImNotListening2025}.

Some popular music scholars have already attempted to capture stylistic patterns in large song lyrics corpora through topic modeling \cite{bansalLinguisticComplexitySociocultural2025} or sentiment analysis \cite{napierQuantitativeSentimentAnalysis2018}; \cite{stoiaBluesLyricFormulas2020} have investigated the use of formulas in blues lyrics, and \cite{aminDatadrivenIdentificationIdioms2021} have developed an automatic classifier for multi-word idioms in German popular music lyrics. However, to our knowledge so far no study has systematically identified and analyzed genre-discriminating stylistic patterns using machine learning models. To this end, n-gram-based models—despite their conceptual simplicity—directly reflect the recurrent lexical patterns that linguists identify as characteristic of popular song language \cite{wernerCatchyConversationalRegister2021}. At the same time, simple n-gram-based features have performed quite well in comparison to more sophisticated hand-crafted style features in lyrics-based genre classification \cite{mayerlComparingLyricsFeatures2020,fell2014lyrics}.

Summing up, this study advances lyrics-based MIR by revisiting the n-gram paradigm through an interpretable machine learning lens. We examine a culturally-bounded large corpus of Anglophone popular songs frequently listened to in Germany to identify content-related, genre-discriminative lyrical patterns including topics, sentiments, and formulaic expressions. In doing so, we test whether pattern-based interpretable models can approximate the predictive power of a fine-tuned transformer baseline while providing substantive interpretive value for popular-music research. Specifically, this paper adresses the following research questions: 

\noindent
\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{@{}Z{0.08\linewidth} Y@{}}
  \textbf{RQ1} & Which lyrical style patterns (topics, sentiments, formulaic expressions) discriminate genres in Anglophone German popular music? \\
  \textbf{RQ2} & To what extent do style-based interpretable ML models match a fine-tuned DistilBERT LLM baseline on lyrics-based music genre prediction? \\
  \textbf{RQ3} & What can we learn about style-related lyrical genre differences from such interpretative models for popular music studies? \\
\end{tabularx}

\subsection{Corpus and Genre Annotations}
\begin{figure}[htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[alt={Corpus description},width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/corpus_descriptive.png}
  \caption{Composition of the analyzed corpus.}
  \label{fig:corpus}
\end{figure}
We based our analysis on a previously collected corpus of 254,334 unique popular tracks frequently listened to in Germany over the past 50 years (POPTRAG). The corpus combines multiple sources: (1)~215,702 tracks from weekly \textit{MediaControl} German single and album charts, capturing the 100 most relevant albums and songs by physical/digital sales, streams, and broadcasts (18 Nov 1977-21 Nov 2024; limited to 75\% of tracks successfully linked to \textit{Spotify} identifiers for consistent metadata); (2) 11,994 unique tracks from Germany's \textit{Daily Top 200 Spotify} charts (Jan 2017–Nov 2024). To capture not only music sales but also listened-to music no longer in the charts, the corpus additionally includes (3) 26,949 \textit{Spotify} track recommendations from 126 German-market genre seeds (retrieved 29 Nov 2024); and (4) 8,801 tracks from the 100 top-featured \textit{Spotify} playlists for Germany (retrieved 29 Nov 2024). 853 tracks shorter than 30 seconds were excluded and only unique title-artist combinations were retained, selecting earlier release dates for duplicates.


Using data linkage and webscraping, we enriched POPTRAG with lyrics from \textit{Genius}\footnote{\url{https://genius.com}} and release music genre annotations from \textit{Discogs}\footnote{\url{https://discogs.com}}. We focused our analysis on tracks with retrieved English lyrics and at least one \textit{Discogs} music genre annotation (51\%). 35\% of the remaining tracks were assinged more than one genre by the \textit{Discogs} community. For them, we retained only the most specific genre label (i.e., the one that appeared least frequently in the corpus) to enable an easily interpretable single-label classification task. Finally, we removed genres that made up less than 1\% of the remaining corpus, resulting in a final sample of N=126,042 tracks across 9 genres (see Figure \ref{fig:corpus} for details on the corpus composition).


\subsection{RQ1: Music Genre Discriminating Vocabularies}
To identify genre-discriminating lyrical style patterns (RQ1), we first performed basic cleaning on raw lyrics from \textit{Genius}: trimming whitespaces, removing content within square brackets, normalizing asterisks, removing repetition and song position indicators, and fixing missing apostrophes (e.g., ``don t'' $\rightarrow$ ``don't'').

To extract relevant n-grams for each lyrical style dimension (topics, sentiments, formulaic expressions), we applied dimension-specific preprocessing steps. For topics and sentiments, we expanded contractions (e.g., ``don t'' $\rightarrow$ ``do not''), lemmatized and POS-tagged lyrics using \textit{spaCy's} \texttt{en\_core\_web\_lg} model with an additional custom domain lexicon, then reduced to nouns (topics) or adjectives and adverbs (sentiments). We retained only tokens with more than two characters present in both the \texttt{en\_core\_web\_lg} vocabulary and the English \textit{nltk} library to exclude non-lexical items. All n-grams were converted to lowercase. For formulaic expressions n-grams, we only accepted sequences that did not cross line breaks or punctuation boundaries.  We then stratified the corpus into 80\% training and 20\% holdout sets by genre distribution, ensuring no artist overlap to mitigate artist and album effects \cite{flexerEffectsAlbumArtist2010}.

We applied the following steps to the training corpus only, reserving the holdout set exclusively for final model evaluation. To construct three separate vocabularies of genre-discriminating n-grams (unigrams for topics and sentiments; unigrams and sequences for formulaic expressions), we discarded n-grams appearing with only a single artist or consisting entirely of stopwords. For expressions specifically, we also excluded bigrams starting with ``a'', ``an'', ``the'', and ``to'' to remove trivial noun+article and to+infinitive constructions.

We employed the fightin' words method \cite{monroeFightinWordsLexical2017} to find genre-discriminative n-grams. This method quantifies n-gram association with each genre via log-odds ratios, comparing relative frequency in one genre against all others combined. We applied Bayesian smoothing using empirical prevalence as a Dirichlet prior, converted the resulting log-odds ratios to z-scores, and applied Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) correction \cite{benjaminiControllingFalseDiscovery1995} at $\alpha = 0.001$ to identify significant overrepresentations.

To extract formulaic expressions, we used a top-down recursive algorithm that iteratively evaluated n-grams from quadgrams to unigrams. At each level it identified significantly discriminative multi-word sequences via FDR-corrected z-scores, glued significant sequences into single tokens in the corpus (resolving overlaps by highest absolute z-score), and then proceeded to the next lower n-gram order on the modified corpus, allowing expansion of glued sequences. In the final phase all resulting unigrams (including glued expressions) were tested; sequences passing the unigram-level FDR were kept as the vocabulary, and non-significant glued phrases were split back into their constituent words to preserve counts.


\subsection{RQ1: Latent Types of Discriminating Patterns}
To identify genre-discriminating latent topics, sentiments, and formulaic-expression types (RQ1), we trained structural topic models (STM) \cite{robertsStmPackageStructural2019} on n-grams from the found vocabularies, using target-genre prevalence as covariates. We aggregated n-gram counts by unique artist–genre combinations (reducing 101,940 track documents to 7,713 artist–genre documents) to mitigate per-track n-gram sparsity, which otherwise increases variance and risks overfitting during STM and subsequent classifier training.

We trained three STMs separately for topics, sentiments, and expressions, testing an interpretable range of 2–20 types each. To select the number of types we used a 50\%/50\% train–validation split and computed validation-set semantic coherence, validation-set likelihood, and exclusivity \cite{robertsStructuralTopicModels2014}; selection prioritized highest exclusivity while retaining good coherence and likelihood. After choosing the number of types, we retrained each STM on the full training corpus and interpreted types using top FREX n-grams (balancing frequency and exclusivity) \cite{bischofSummarizingTopicalContent2012}. Finally, we applied the final models to tracks to obtain per-track type probabilities. For insights into the distribution of types in the training data, we averaged those probabilities per genre in the training set.


\subsection{RQ2 and RQ3: Machine Learning Experiments}
To evaluate how well genre-discriminating lyrical styles predict music genre (RQ2), we trained machine learning classifiers using the extracted topic, sentiment, and expression type probabilities as features, separately per dimension and combined. We removed redundant features by dropping, for each STM, the type with the highest summed probability across artist–genre aggregated documents (to avoid multicollinearity since type probabilities sum to 1 per document), preserving the full information content.

We initially trained elastic-net logistic regression models (GLMNET) \cite{defazioSAGAFastIncremental2014} with fixed hyperparameters (penalty $C=1$, L1 ratio $=0.5$) on individual and combined feature sets. Additionally, we performed systematic hyperparameter tuning for the best-performing feature set with GLMNET (20×20 grid search, $C$ logarithmic in $[10^{-3},10^{3}]$, L1 ratio in $[0,1]$) and LightGBM \cite{keLightGBMHighlyEfficient2017a} (Tree-structured Parzen Estimator, TPE \cite{optuna2019}; trees in $[100,2000]$, learning rate in $[0.001,0.1]$, leaves in $[20,70]$, max depth in $[1,10]$, minimum leaf data in $[1,100]$, feature fraction in $[0.1,1.0]$, $L1$/$L2$ regularization logarithmic in $[10^{-8},10^{1}]$) using 4-fold artist-grouped stratified cross-validation. We evaluated training performance from cross-validation and final prediction performance on a holdout set (untouched by model training or feature extraction) via macro $F_{1}$.

We compared the performance of our models against a random baseline, a TF-IDF \cite{fell2014lyrics} + GLMNET pipeline, and a fine-tuned DistilBERT model \cite{sanhDistilBERTDistilledVersion2020} trained on raw lyrics with tuned hyperparamters (TPE; 20 iterations with stratified 80/20\% train-validation split; tuned learning rate [10$^{-5}$, $10^{4}$]; batch size 8, 16, or 32; weight decay [0, 0.3]; warmup ratio [0, 0.2]; and number of epochs 2, 3, or 4). To identify which stylistic patterns most contributed to genre discrimination and to investigate interpretable genre profiles (RQ3), we computed permutation feature importance \cite{fisherAllModelsAre2019} on the holdout set for the best combined-features model and calculated SHAP values \cite{lundbergUnifiedApproachInterpreting2017a} on the holdout set for the best-performing genre to illustrate how specific features drive predictions.

Using genre-discriminative topics, sentiments, and expression types as lyrical-style features, we trained GLMNET and LightGBM models to predict song genre, and compared performance to a random baseline, TF-IDF+GLMNET, and a fine-tuned DistilBERT on raw lyrics (Table~\ref{tab:f1}). Within the artist–genre aggregated documents used to train the STM, the topic \textit{Struggle}, the sentiment \textit{vulnerable}, and the expression \textit{Denying \& Negotiating} had the highest summed probabilities and were therefore chosen as base classes and discarded for subsequent machine-learning experiments. Our structured representations provided informative genre signal compared to the random baseline (holdout $F_{1}=0.111$) and TF-IDF+GLMNET (0.248). Among structured features, expressions were most discriminative ($F_{1}=0.317$), followed by topics ($F_{1}=0.232$) and sentiments ($F_{1}=0.154$). Combined features with GLMNET ($C=1$, L1$=0.5$) achieved holdout macro $F_{1}=0.316$, not exceeding expressions alone. Hyperparameter tuning did not improve GLMNET with expression features on the holdout (CV mean $F_{1}=0.371$, holdout $F_{1}=0.316$). The tuned LightGBM with expression features performed slightly better (CV mean $F_{1}=0.380$, holdout $F_{1}=0.318$). 

Although DistilBERT remained strongest overall ($F_{1}=0.382$), the tuned LightGBM retained 83.2\% of its predictive power while offering interpretable features and contributions. The tuned LightGBM model performs best for \textit{Hip Hop} (holdout $F1$=0.632) and \textit{Rock} (0.611), and worst for \textit{Folk, World \& Country} (0.159) and \textit{Jazz} (0.152) (see Figure \ref{fig:confusion_matrix}). Genre prevalence in the training data shows a weak positive relationship with classification quality (Kendall correlation = 0.222), while some smaller genres like \textit{Hip Hop} outperform larger ones like \textit{Pop}.

A few expression types show increased holdout $F_{1}$ when permuted—likely reflecting feature sparsity or communality rather than substantive global genre relevance (\textit{Depressive Expressions}, \textit{Struggle Expressions}, \textit{Christmas Phrases}, and \textit{Hedonistic Celebration}).

\section{Discussion}
In this contribution, we identify genre-discriminative lyrical style patterns in Anglophone popular music frequently listened to in Germany (RQ1): 12 topics, 8 sentiments, and 15 types of formulaic expressions. Interpretable machine-learning models based on these patterns (RQ2) retain 83,2\% of the predictive performance of a fine-tuned DistilBERT classifier, demonstrating that pattern-based features allow effective and interpretable genre classification. Machine-learning experiments and final feature-importance analyses (RQ3) reveal that in general formulaic expressions are substantially more discriminative than topics and sentiments, indicating this aspect has been largely underestimated in prior research. Using \textit{Hip Hop} as an example, we demonstrate how expressions, topics, and sentiments jointly characterize the genre’s lyrical style.

In sum, lyrical genre differences affect not only lexical, diversity, readability, rhyme, and structural aspects \cite{paradacabaleiroSongLyricsHave2024,mayerlComparingLyricsFeatures2020,schedlGenreDifferencesSong2019,fell2014lyrics}, but also style as represented by formulaic epxression and topical patterns, and to a lesser extent sentiments (RQ1). Formulaic expressions are especially informative for genre differentiation compared to topics and sentiments, supporting the view that genre-specific phrasing substantially shapes how genres are lyrically defined and perceived \cite{frithWhySongsHave1986}. Consistent with prior findings \cite{brandCulturalEvolutionEmotional2019,napierQuantitativeSentimentAnalysis2018}, we observe a high prevalence of negatively valenced topics, sentiments, and expressions (e.g., \textit{Struggle}, \textit{vulnerable}, \textit{Denying \& Negotiating}).


In our interpretable machine-learning experiments (RQ2), Hip Hop and Rock were most readily identifiable from lyrical features alone, suggesting some genres rely more strongly on distinctive lyrical style markers while others share similar markers and are therefore more easily confused. Given that \textit{Discogs' Rock} encompasses metal styles, this finding aligns with prior lyric-based genre work \cite{tsaptsinosLyricsBasedMusicGenre2017, fell2014lyrics} and the view of music genre as rather fuzzy categories organized around prototypical features that not only encompass lyrics, but also acoustical, release, and artist features \cite{fabbriTheoryMusicalGenres1982}, which vary in importance for distinction depending on the genre \cite{fell2014lyrics}. 

Our feature-importance analysis (RQ3) showed that certain formulaic expressions dominate genre distinctions in our corpus (e.g., \textit{Dance Shoutings, Old School Street Slang, Violent Assault}), while others did not generalize to holdout data. An exemplary analysis of the \textit{Discogs Hip Hop} genre profile revealed a mix of strong style markers and weaker, context-dependent interactions. Although the STM was trained on artist-genre-aggregated documents, the learned n-gram distributions capture stable stylistic regularities that generalize beyond the training level. Individual tracks' lyrics can be seen as very short, noisy realizations of these latent styles, enabling meaningful posterior inference of type probabilities at the track level, as benchmarking results show.

Beyond insights into Anglophone German popular music, our findings demonstrate a principled distinction between predictive and explanatory machine-learning approaches: black-box transformers excel at prediction, while interpretable models reveal which linguistic patterns drive those predictions, enabling musicological insights into what constitutes genres rather than merely optimizing genre tagging \cite{gardaziBERTApplicationsNatural2025}. This explanatory capacity supports practical applications such as diagnosing genre biases in generative models that use lyrics \cite{rudinStopExplainingBlack2019}, corpus-curation tools that flag culturally distinctive markers for ethnomusicological analyses \cite{stoiaBluesLyricFormulas2020}, and hybrid genre taggers for streaming platforms that balance predictive power with transparency.

Our analysis faces several limitations. First, music genres are inherently fuzzy categories \cite{vanvenrooijCategoricalAmbiguityCultural2018}; by using \textit{Discogs} labels we adopt a particular community’s perspective on genre boundaries, and using alternative category systems could yield different findings. Second, we intentionally simplified a larger number of multi-labeled tracks to single-label classification to enable a simpler prediction approach and greater interpretability. Third, permutation feature importance can be biased for highly correlated features \cite{molnarInterpretableMachineLearning2020}. Although some features showed substantial intercorrelations, we preserved individual linguistic pattern interpretability at the cost of potential ranking distortion. Fourth, our approach treats genres as stable categories, whereas some authors argued that meanings may change over time \cite{lenaClassificationCultureTypes2008}. Finally, our corpus focuses on Anglophone popular music consumed in Germany, limiting generalizability to global Anglophone repertoires or non-English music popular in Germany.

Future work will extend the presented approach to other languages to compare lyrical patterns across linguistic contexts and explore language-agnostic interpretable models \cite{wernerEnglishGermanPop2023}. We will also test generalizability on diverse corpora beyond German popular-music listening and incorporate additional lyrical features alongside multimodal audio characteristics to build more robust interpretable genre classifiers.
